1. My first graph shows a bar chart of the number of games played in 10 year increments from 1975 through 2015. I chose these years because they do a very good job in showing the gradual increase in number of games played over time. I thought that 1975 was a good starting point, as games prior to this year seemed outdated.

My second graph shows a scatter plot of the top 10 countries in international football based on winning percentage. I chose to show the number of games played on the y axis in order to draw attention to countries with a high winning percentage with many games played, a feat that shows that country's strength. Additionally, I set a 100 game minimum in order to exclude countries that have played very few games that have a high winning percentage, as these wins do not necessarily signify strength and these countries do not deserve to be included in this graph based on a handful of wins. This graph features a tooltip -- you can see the country that a dot represents as well as that country's winning percentage and games played by putting your mouse over it.

My third graph shows the strongest teams in each of the last two World Cups. There are two buttons for the 2014 and 2018 World Cups, and each of these two graphs feature different countries and different scores based on performance in this specific World Cup. The score I include as a metric of performance is calculated as follows: each country earns an amount of points equal to their opposition's all time winning percentage for beating that team, and an amount of points equal to one half of theiropposition's all time winning percentage for tying that team. I chose to calculate score in this way because a country's score will increase more when beating historically good teams than beating historically bad teams, and will increase by more winning a game than tying or losing a game. Losing a game grants that country 0 points. I chose not to make this score an average based on games played because the World Cup is played in a knockout format, which means that the teams that win games will advance and thus play more games, which can only increase their score. Thus, teams that advance will end up with a higher score, all else equal, due to playing more games and winning those games than the teams that lose and do not advance.

You can easily alter your code and see the resulting graph, and make changes until you are satisfied with the end product.
